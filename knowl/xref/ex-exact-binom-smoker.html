Example 4.3.11. Smoker survey probability.

Approximately 15% of the US population smokes cigarettes. A local government believed their community had a lower smoker rate and commissioned a survey of 400 randomly selected individuals. The survey found that only 42 of the 400 participants smoke cigarettes. If the true proportion of smokers in the community was really 15%, what is the probability of observing 42 or fewer smokers in a sample of 400 people?

The question posed is equivalent to asking, what is the probability of observing \(k=0\text{,}\) 1, 2, â€¦, or 42 smokers in a sample of \(n=400\) when \(p=0.15\text{?}\) We can compute these 43 different probabilities and add them together to find the answer:
\begin{align*} \amp P(k=0 \text{ or } k=1 \text{ or } \cdots \text{ or } k=42)\\ \amp \quad = P(k=0) + P(k=1) + \cdots + P(k=42)\\ \amp \quad = 0.0054 \end{align*}
If the true proportion of smokers in the community is \(p=0.15\text{,}\) then the probability of observing 42 or fewer smokers in a sample of \(n=400\) is 0.0054.
