Section 1.5 Review exercises

Exercises Review exercises

1. Pet names.

Seattle pet registry data show proportions of cats and dogs with each name. A scatterplot compares name popularity.

  1. Experiment or observational study?

  2. Most common dog and cat names?

  3. Which names are more common for cats than dogs?

  4. Is the relationship positive or negative? Interpret.

2. Stressed out, Part II.

Half of subjects are randomly assigned to a stressful falling-elevator experience; half to no stress. Determine study type and whether causation can be concluded.

3. Chia seeds and weight loss.

38 men and 38 women randomly assigned to chia seeds or placebo for 12 weeks; volunteers. No significant differences found.

  1. Study type?

  2. Experimental and control treatments?

  3. Was blocking used? If so, what variable?

  4. Was blinding used?

  5. Can we make causal statements? Can we generalize?

4. City council survey.

Household survey across diverse neighborhoods. For each sampling plan (simple random; stratified by neighborhood; cluster; multistage; convenience), name the method and discuss pros/cons.

5. Flawed reasoning.

Identify flaws and improvements in three scenarios: parent questionnaires on work schedule; follow-up survey with attrition; doctor surveying runners about joint problems.

6. Income and education in US counties.

Scatterplot of per capita income vs. percent with bachelor’s degree for US counties.

  1. Identify explanatory and response variables.

  2. Describe the relationship and any unusual observations.

  3. Can we conclude having a bachelor’s degree increases income?

7. Eat better, feel better.

Randomized study of three interventions to increase fruit/vegetable intake among university students; only the group given servings showed improved psychological well-being.

  1. Study type?

  2. Explanatory and response variables?

  3. Can results be generalized?

  4. Can results establish causality?

  5. Revise a headline claiming “proof” accordingly.

8. Screens, teens, and psychological well-being.

Large observational diary study of screen time and well-being for teens (n=17,247) with covariates. Concluded little clear evidence of harm.

  1. Study type?

  2. Explanatory variables?

  3. Response variable?

  4. Can results be generalized? Why?

  5. Can results establish causation?

9. Stanford Open Policing.

Summary table shows stops per year, search and arrest rates by county, state, and driver race.

  1. List variables collected per traffic stop.

  2. Classify each variable (numerical/categorical; continuous/discrete; ordinal).

  3. For analyzing search rates by race, identify response and explanatory variables.

10. Space launches.

Table shows launch outcomes by agency type (Private, State, Startup) and era (1957-1999, 2000-2018).

  1. Variables collected per launch?

  2. Classify each variable.

  3. For studying success rates across agencies and time, identify response and explanatory variables.
