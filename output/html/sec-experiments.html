    Section 1.4 Experiments

    In experiments, researchers assign treatments to cases. Randomized experiments are the gold standard for establishing causation.

    Subsection 1.4.1 Principles of experimental design

    Four core principles:
    Controlling: hold other factors constant across groups. Randomization: randomly assign cases to treatments to balance unmeasured factors. Replication: use enough cases (and replicate studies) to estimate effects precisely. Blocking: when a known factor affects the response, group by that factor and randomize within blocks.

    Subsection 1.4.2 Reducing bias in human experiments

    To avoid placebo and observer effects, patients are blinded to treatment via placebos; double-blind designs also blind caregivers/researchers interacting with patients.

    Checkpoint 1.4.2. Blinding in the stent study.

    Was the stent study an experiment? Was it blind or double-blind?
    Solution.
    It was an experiment (researchers assigned treatments). It was not blind, so not double-blind.

    Checkpoint 1.4.3. Placebos via sham surgery.

    Could the stent study have used a placebo? What would it look like?
    Solution.
    A sham surgery‚Äîperforming surgical steps without implanting the stent‚Äîcould serve as a placebo, though with ethical considerations.

    Exercises 1.4.3 Exercises

    1. Light and exam performance.

    Design: compare lighting types on exam performance, blocking by sex. Identify response, explanatory variable (with levels), and blocking variable.

    2. Vitamin supplements.

    Study with placebo, 1g, 3g vitamin C, 3g vitamin C plus additives; appearance identical. Nurses know assignments; assessors are blinded. No significant differences found.
    1. Experiment or observational?
    2. Explanatory and response variables?
    3. Were patients blinded?
    4. Was it double-blind?
    5. Does non-adherence introduce confounding?

    3. Light, noise, and exam performance.

    Design tests light (3 levels) and noise (3 levels) on exam performance, blocking by sex.
    1. What type of study?
    2. How many factors? List factors and levels.
    3. Role of sex variable?

    4. Music and learning.

    Outline an experiment comparing study with no music, instrumental music, or music with lyrics.

    5. Soda preference.

    Outline an experiment to compare regular Coke vs. Diet Coke preference.

    6. Exercise and mental health.

    A stratified randomized study assigns half of each age group (18-30, 31-40, 41-55) to exercise twice a week and half to no exercise; mental health measured pre/post.
    1. Study type?
    2. Treatment and control groups?
    3. Is blocking used? What variable?
    4. Is blinding used?
    5. Can results establish causation and generalize? Explain.
    6. Any reservations about funding this study?
