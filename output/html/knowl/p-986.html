If the probability Brian kicks a 35 yard field goal is \(p=0.8\text{,}\) what is the probability it takes Brian exactly six tries to get his fourth successful kick? We can write this as
\begin{align*} \amp P(\text{it takes Brian six tries to make four field goals})\\ \amp \quad = P(\text{Brian makes three of his first five field goals, and he makes the sixth one})\\ \amp \quad = P(\text{$1^{st}$ sequence OR $2^{nd}$ sequence OR } \cdots \text{ OR $10^{th}$ sequence}) \end{align*}
where the sequences are from Figure 4.4.4. We can break down this last probability into the sum of ten disjoint possibilities:
\begin{align*} \amp P(\text{$1^{st}$ sequence OR $2^{nd}$ sequence OR } \cdots \text{ OR $10^{th}$ sequence})\\ \amp \quad = P(\text{$1^{st}$ sequence}) + P(\text{$2^{nd}$ sequence}) + \cdots + P(\text{$10^{th}$ sequence}) \end{align*}
The probability of the first sequence was identified in Checkpoint 4.4.5 as 0.0164, and each of the other sequences have the same probability. Since each of the ten sequences has the same probability, the total probability is ten times that of any individual sequence.
