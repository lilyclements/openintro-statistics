    Section 1.4 Experiments

    In experiments, researchers assign treatments to cases. Randomized experiments are the gold standard for establishing causation.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.4.1 Principles of experimental design

    Four core principles:
    🔗
    Controlling: hold other factors constant across groups. Randomization: randomly assign cases to treatments to balance unmeasured factors. Replication: use enough cases (and replicate studies) to estimate effects precisely. Blocking: when a known factor affects the response, group by that factor and randomize within blocks.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.4.2 Reducing bias in human experiments

    To avoid placebo and observer effects, patients are blinded to treatment via placebos; double-blind designs also blind caregivers/researchers interacting with patients.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.4.2. Blinding in the stent study.

    Was the stent study an experiment? Was it blind or double-blind?
    🔗
    Solution.
    It was an experiment (researchers assigned treatments). It was not blind, so not double-blind.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.4.3. Placebos via sham surgery.

    Could the stent study have used a placebo? What would it look like?
    🔗
    Solution.
    A sham surgery—performing surgical steps without implanting the stent—could serve as a placebo, though with ethical considerations.
    🔗
    🔗
